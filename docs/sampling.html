4 Sampling and Research Methods

4.1 Overview

In sport business analytics, we rarely have access to data from every individual in a population. Instead, we collect data from a sample - a subset of the population - and use statistical methods to draw conclusions about the larger group. Understanding proper sampling techniques is critical for conducting valid research and making sound business decisions.

This chapter covers:

  • Why sampling is necessary in sport analytics
  • Key sampling terminology
  • Probability vs.Â non-probability sampling methods
  • Sampling bias and how to avoid it
  • Validity and reliability in measurement

4.2 The Role of Sampling in Analytics

4.2.1 What is Statistics?

Statistics is the process of collecting, analyzing, and interpreting data to produce concise, readily consumable information that supports decision-making.

In sport business, we use statistics to answer questions like:

  • What is the average ticket price across all NBA games?
  • How satisfied are season ticket holders with their experience?
  • Which marketing campaigns generate the highest ROI?
  • What factors predict game attendance?

Critical decisions are made based on samples. Proper sampling enables us to:

  1. Gather a small and manageable bundle of data from a population of interest
  2. Statistically process those data
  3. Reasonably comprehend the larger population from which it was drawn

4.2.2 Why Sample Instead of Measuring Everyone?

There are four main reasons we use sampling in sport analytics:

4.2.2.1 1. Time

Collecting data from an entire population is time-consuming. For example:

  • Surveying every fan who attended games this season could take months
  • By that time, the information may no longer be relevant for decision-making
  • Sampling allows us to gather insights quickly and act on them while theyâre still useful

Time-sensitive decisions in sport business (ticket pricing adjustments, promotional campaign changes, etc.) require rapid data collection that only sampling can provide.

4.2.2.2 2. Cost

Data collection costs money:

  • Surveys: Printing, postage, web platform fees, incentives for participants
  • Experiments: Recruitment advertising, participant compensation, facility rental
  • Staff time: People to distribute surveys, conduct interviews, process responses

Sampling reduces costs by limiting the number of people from whom we collect data, making research projects feasible within budget constraints.

4.2.2.3 3. Feasibility

Some populations are simply too large or geographically dispersed to measure completely:

  • All NFL fans in the United States (millions of people across 50 states)
  • Every person who has ever attended a college basketball game
  • All youth soccer players in North America

Even if we had unlimited time and money, logistical constraints make population-wide data collection impractical. Sampling makes these investigations feasible.

4.2.2.4 4. Extrapolation

Hereâs the key insight: By sampling properly, it is unnecessary to gather data on an entire population to achieve a reasonable comprehension of it.

Extrapolation involves using sampling methods and statistics to analyze a sample, then extending those findings to understand the larger population. When done correctly, a well-designed sample of 400 people can accurately represent a population of 40,000.

4.3 Sampling Terminology

Before diving into sampling methods, letâs define key terms youâll encounter:

4.3.1 Population

The population is the entire realm of people (or entities) that could be measured or counted for your research question.

Examples:

  • All currently enrolled students at a specified university
  • All registered voters in an election district
  • All season ticket holders for an MLB team
  • All members of a professional sports league
  • All employees of a sport organization

Key word: âAllâ - The population includes every member of the group of interest.

4.3.2 Sample Frame

The sample frame (or sampling frame) is the part of the population you could potentially access for data collection.

For example:

  • You want to study UNF students (population)
  • The registrar provides a list of all currently enrolled students with email addresses (sample frame)
  • However, some students opted out of having their information shared
  • Your sample frame is smaller than the full population, but itâs the list you can actually work with

The sample frame represents your accessible population.

4.3.3 Sample

The sample is a subset of individuals selected from the sample frame for your study.

Example:

  • Population: 16,000 UNF students
  • Sample Frame: 14,500 students who didnât opt out of research participation
  • Sample: 300 students randomly selected from the sample frame

4.4 Types of Sampling Methods

Sampling methods fall into two broad categories: probability sampling and non-probability sampling.

4.4.1 Probability Sampling

In probability sampling, each element in the sample frame has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. This is also called âequal opportunity samplingâ because every member has the same probability of selection.

Probability sampling methods include:

  • Simple random sampling
  • Systematic sampling
  • Stratified sampling
  • Proportionate and disproportionate sampling
  • Cluster (area) sampling

Advantage: Probability sampling allows us to make statistical inferences about the population and calculate margins of error.

4.4.2 Non-Probability Sampling

In non-probability sampling, elements are not selected randomly. Instead, theyâre chosen based on:

  • Convenience (whoâs easy to reach)
  • Specific characteristics (purposive selection)
  • Referrals from other participants (snowball sampling)
  • Quotas that need to be filled

Non-probability sampling methods include:

  • Convenience sampling
  • Purposive (judgment) sampling
  • Quota sampling
  • Snowball sampling

Limitation: Non-probability samples sacrifice external validity - the ability to generalize findings from your sample to the overall population. However, theyâre often more practical and less expensive.

4.5 Probability Sampling Methods

Letâs examine each probability sampling method in detail:

4.5.1 Simple Random Sampling

Simple random sampling randomly selects a specific number of elements from the sample frame, where each element has an equal chance of selection.

Think of it as drawing names from a hat - every name has the same probability of being drawn.

Example in Sport Analytics:

You want to survey 200 season ticket holders about their satisfaction. You have a list of 2,000 season ticket holders. You use a random number generator to select 200 of them.

In R, you might use:

# Create sample frame of 2000 season ticket holders
season_ticket_holders <- data.frame(
  ID = 1:2000,
  Name = paste("STH", 1:2000)
)

# Simple random sample of 200
set.seed(123)  # For reproducibility
sample_ids <- sample(season_ticket_holders$ID, size = 200)
selected_sample <- season_ticket_holders[season_ticket_holders$ID %in% sample_ids, ]

4.5.2 Systematic Sampling

Systematic sampling uses a periodic selection process that draws the sample more evenly throughout the sample frame.

Steps:

  1. Calculate the skip interval (k): k = Sample Frame Size Ã· Target Sample Size
  2. Randomly select a starting number between 1 and k
  3. Select every k-th element after that

Example:

  • Sample frame: 1,000 fans
  • Target sample: 100 fans
  • Skip interval: k = 1000 Ã· 100 = 10
  • Random start: 7
  • Select fans #7, 17, 27, 37, 47, â¦ , 997
# Systematic sampling
sample_frame_size <- 1000
target_sample_size <- 100

k <- sample_frame_size / target_sample_size  # Skip interval = 10
start <- sample(1:k, 1)  # Random start between 1 and 10

systematic_sample <- seq(from = start, to = sample_frame_size, by = k)

When to use: Systematic sampling ensures even representation across the sample frame, which is useful when the frame is ordered in some meaningful way (e.g., alphabetically, chronologically).

4.5.3 Stratified Sampling

Stratified sampling divides the sample frame into homogeneous subgroups (strata) and then randomly selects from each stratum.

Why stratify? To ensure adequate representation of important subgroups.

Example:

Youâre studying fan satisfaction across three conference divisions:

  • Power 5 schools: 65 schools
  • Group of 5 schools: 65 schools
  • FCS schools: 120 schools

Instead of simple random sampling (which might under-represent smaller groups), you stratify by division and sample from each:

# Stratified sampling example
library(dplyr)

# Sample frame with strata
schools <- data.frame(
  School = paste("School", 1:250),
  Division = c(rep("Power 5", 65), rep("Group of 5", 65), rep("FCS", 120))
)

# Sample 30 from each stratum
stratified_sample <- schools %>%
  group_by(Division) %>%
  sample_n(30)

4.5.4 Proportionate vs.Â Disproportionate Sampling

When using stratified sampling, you can sample either:

Proportionate sampling: Select the same percentage from each stratum

Example: Sample 10% from each division (6-7 from Power 5, 6-7 from Group of 5, 12 from FCS)

Disproportionate sampling: Select the same number from each stratum

Example: Sample 30 from each division (even though they have different sizes)

Use disproportionate sampling when you want to ensure sufficient sample size in smaller strata for meaningful analysis.

4.5.5 Cluster (Area) Sampling

Cluster sampling (also called multistage cluster sampling) is used when:

  • The population is geographically dispersed
  • A complete sample frame doesnât exist
  • You want to reduce travel costs for in-person data collection

Instead of sampling individuals, you sample clusters (groups) and then collect data from everyone within selected clusters, or sample within clusters.

Example:

Studying youth soccer players in Florida:

  1. Stage 1: Randomly select 10 counties from Floridaâs 67 counties
  2. Stage 2: Randomly select 3 soccer leagues within each selected county
  3. Stage 3: Survey all players in selected leagues (or sample within leagues)
# Two-stage cluster sampling
# Stage 1: Select counties
florida_counties <- paste("County", 1:67)
selected_counties <- sample(florida_counties, 10)

# Stage 2: Within each county, select leagues
# (This would require a data frame with leagues nested in counties)

When to use: Cluster sampling is most efficient for geographically dispersed populations or when creating a complete sampling frame is impractical.

4.6 Non-Probability Sampling Methods

While probability sampling is ideal for statistical inference, non-probability methods are often more practical in real-world sport business settings.

4.6.1 Convenience Sampling

Convenience sampling (also called availability sampling) recruits whoever is relevant and readily accessible.

Example:

You want to survey fans about their concession preferences. You stand outside the stadium and survey people as they enter. Your sample consists of whoever happens to walk by and agrees to participate.

Advantages: - Quick and inexpensive - Easy to implement - Useful for pilot studies or exploratory research

Disadvantages: - High risk of sampling bias - Cannot generalize to the population - May over-represent certain groups (e.g., early arrivers)

4.6.2 Purposive (Judgment) Sampling

Purposive sampling involves deliberately selecting participants who meet specific criteria that are important for your study.

Example:

Youâre studying the effectiveness of a new training program for elite athletes. You purposefully select:

  • Athletes who completed the full program
  • Athletes aged 18-25
  • Athletes in track and field
  • Athletes with no history of major injuries

Youâre not trying to represent all athletes - youâre specifically studying this particular subgroup.

When to use:

  • When the characteristics youâre interested in are rare in the population
  • For in-depth case studies
  • When you need expert participants
  • In qualitative research

Example in Sport Business:

Studying sport analytics career paths by interviewing 20 analytics directors from professional teams. You purposefully select directors with 5+ years of experience who lead analytics departments.

4.6.3 Quota Sampling

Quota sampling sets specific quotas for subgroups and fills them with any available participants from each group.

Itâs similar to stratified sampling, but without random selection.

Example:

Survey about NIL (Name, Image, Likeness) deals. You set quotas:

  • 25 male athletes with NIL deals
  • 25 female athletes with NIL deals
  • 25 male athletes without NIL deals
  • 25 female athletes without NIL deals

You survey athletes until each quota is filled. Once a quota is met, you stop recruiting from that group.

When to use: When you need quick results and want to ensure representation of key subgroups, but donât have the resources for stratified random sampling.

4.6.4 Snowball Sampling

Snowball sampling starts with a few participants who then refer other potential participants from their network.

Process:

  1. Identify initial participants who meet your criteria
  2. Ask them to refer others who meet the criteria
  3. Contact the referrals and ask them to participate
  4. Ask new participants for more referrals
  5. Continue until you reach your target sample size

Example:

Studying experiences of former professional athletes who transitioned to coaching. You:

  • Start with 5 former athletes you can identify
  • Ask each to recommend 2-3 others in similar situations
  • The sample âsnowballsâ as each participant refers others

When to use:

  • For hard-to-reach or âhiddenâ populations
  • When no sampling frame exists
  • For sensitive topics where trust and referrals are important

Limitation: âBirds of a feather flock togetherâ - your sample may be highly homogeneous because people tend to know others similar to themselves.

4.7 Sampling Bias

Sampling bias occurs when certain members of the population are more (or less) likely to be selected for the sample, leading to a sample that doesnât accurately represent the population.

4.7.1 Sources of Sampling Bias

  1. Selection method bias: How you recruit participants affects who responds

    • Online-only survey excludes people without internet access
    • Phone survey excludes people without phones or who donât answer unknown numbers
    • Stadium intercept survey over-represents fans who arrive early

  2. Self-selection bias: People who volunteer may differ systematically from non-volunteers

    • Satisfaction surveys often over-represent very satisfied or very dissatisfied customers
    • Online polls attract people with strong opinions

  3. Location bias: Where you collect data affects whoâs included

    • Surveying only at premium seating areas misses general admission fans
    • Collecting data only at home games misses traveling supporters

  4. Timing bias: When you collect data affects participation

    • Weekday surveys miss people who work
    • End-of-season surveys miss fans who stopped attending

4.7.2 Consequences of Sampling Bias

Biased samples lead to:

  • Inaccurate estimates of population parameters
  • Wrong conclusions about relationships between variables
  • Poor business decisions based on misleading data
  • Wasted resources on ineffective strategies

4.7.3 Reducing Sampling Bias

While it may not be possible to eliminate all bias, you can reduce it by:

  1. Using probability sampling methods when possible
  2. Carefully defining your population and sample frame
  3. Considering multiple data collection methods (online + phone + mail)
  4. Documenting your sampling procedures so others can assess potential biases
  5. Comparing your sample demographics to known population characteristics
  6. Being transparent about limitations in your conclusions

Example:

If your fan satisfaction survey over-represents season ticket holders (who tend to be more satisfied), acknowledge this limitation and avoid claiming the results represent all fans.

4.8 Validity and Reliability

Before collecting data, you must ensure your measurements are both valid and reliable.

4.8.1 Validity

Validity refers to whether your measure actually measures what itâs intended to measure.

Example Questions:

  • Does your âfan engagementâ survey really measure engagement, or does it measure something else (like satisfaction or loyalty)?
  • Do ticket sales reflect fan interest, or do they reflect ticket pricing and promotion effectiveness?

Types of Validity:

  1. Content validity: Does the measure cover all aspects of the concept?

    • A fan engagement survey should cover multiple dimensions: attendance, social media interaction, merchandise purchases, watching/streaming games, etc.

  2. Criterion-related validity: Does the measure correlate with other measures it should correlate with?

    • Fan engagement scores should correlate with actual attendance and merchandise sales

  3. Construct validity: Does the measure behave as theory predicts?

    • If your theory says engagement increases with team success, do your engagement scores increase when the team wins?

  4. External validity: Can you generalize findings from your sample to the population?

    • This is threatened by non-probability sampling and sampling bias

4.8.2 Reliability

Reliability refers to the consistency of your measurement - the degree to which an instrument produces the same results when used repeatedly under the same conditions.

Think of reliability like a scale: - A reliable scale shows 150 lbs every time you step on it - An unreliable scale shows 148 lbs, then 152 lbs, then 146 lbs for the same person

Types of Reliability:

  1. Test-retest reliability: Does the measure give consistent results over time?

    • If you measure fan satisfaction today and again next week (with no games between), do you get similar results?

  2. Inter-rater reliability: Do different observers/raters produce consistent results?

    • If two researchers code fan social media posts for sentiment, do they reach similar conclusions?

  3. Internal consistency: Do items measuring the same concept produce consistent results?

    • If your engagement survey has 10 questions about engagement, do people who score high on one question tend to score high on others?

4.8.3 The Relationship Between Validity and Reliability

Important principles:

  • A measure can be reliable but not valid (consistently measuring the wrong thing)
  • A measure cannot be valid without being reliable (if itâs inconsistent, it canât be measuring what you want)
  • Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity

4.8.4 Assessing Validity and Reliability

Before using any measurement instrument (survey, test, observation protocol):

  1. Review existing literature: Has this measure been validated by previous researchers?
  2. Pilot test: Try it with a small sample to identify problems
  3. Calculate reliability statistics: Cronbachâs alpha for internal consistency
  4. Compare to established measures: Do results align with known valid measures?
  5. Seek expert review: Have subject matter experts evaluate content validity
